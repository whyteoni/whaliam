Who/What is a Whaliam Wallace?

Many years back now, somewhere in the early 2010s, I had a thought pop in my head. This was a time of vampires, werewolves, and increasingly other were-beasts. Underwold and True Blood had not quite given way to The Walking Dead and Game of Thrones or Stranger Things. The increasing pantheon of were-beasts (werecats, werebears, and so on) lead to an obvious fascination, “What other creatures could be given the were- treatment?” And in the tempest of that context I had an image resolve in my head: a werewhale. More specifically I had an image of a were-humpback-whale (becuase Star Trek 4: A Voyage Home was my go to movie as a kid). It was in the ocean, racing to the shore, trying to outrun the sun as it crested the horizon behind it. The werewhale had already begun to change, and his powerful fluke had returned to the naked legs of a man, massively disproportionate to the rest of the body. He wasn’t going to make it.

This paradoxical creature entrigued me. In the fashion of a reductio ad absurdum inquest I conjured scenarios that, to me, seemed hilarious, and also tested the theoretical bounds of the were- mythos. What happens if a were-beast dies while transformed? Do their remains stay in same form the person/create was in at the moment of death? Are there were-humans, beasts who turn into humans during the full moon? These and many more questions and their, often grotesque, answers became a recurring theme in the random spaces the mind wastes time in when the Wi-Fi is bad in the doctor’s lobby.

Of all the beasts imagined though, none stayed with me more than the first, the werewhale I named William, no… Whaliam. And if you are a William fighting for the survival of your kind, mythical or otherwise, you might as well be a Wallace, and thus was crafted the idea of Whalium Wallace.
